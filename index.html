DH1001 Digital Artefact Creation

Our group had decided on a 3D model as we were unsure of what to do, and 3D modelling had piqued our interest. Scanning was the most accessible method for us to use. As well as easy to make for us. It also gave us insight into the intricacies of 3D modelling.

Scanning gave us an easy method to precure 3D models, which also had various setbacks and issues we had to overcome.

With issues such as lighting, angles, and more came up, we eventually got 3D models that satisfied us to present. This showed us the importance of accessiblity online, as well as lack of information for certain statues.

My role in this project was to help make the 3D models more accessible and help deal with unexpected problems that arrived. The only one in the group who couldn’t open the .usdz file was me. The model first had to be given to me as a .zip file, so I could then email both files to my school email. Then, I could access both files on my laptop, where I began fixing this issue.

First, attempting to run the model through blender and converting the file did not work. A lot of attempts made failed, as I began to lose hope in the model, as it appears to only work on certain products. After going to a file conversion website, I attempted to convert all the files in the ZIP to a PNG. This worked and allowed us to show said model in our presentation and for all to see.

There was also an issue with a statue in UCC, where there was no information on her, however we were unable to find anything about this woman statue. Due to this, we did not use her as our final product.

DH1002 MapSwipe/OpenStreetMap

Mapswipe was the more difficult of the two to use. The lack of a tutorial or introduction in the app itself allowed for a lot of confusion to be made. There are only two tabs you are able to select from in the app itself; projects and profile. The multiple projects available which you can join and back, many of which are admirable and show the dedication humanitarians possess to help and understand the world we live in. In order to edit and contribute towards Mapswipe, you must fine the website which is located under your profile. From here, you are capable of viewing what others had added, on top of editing the map yourself. I had used Mapswipe to investigate what was added in my local area and add specific points where nothing was added. OpenStreetMap is on the complete opposite side of the spectrum, being the easier of the two to use. There is a brief tutorial on the website itself which allowed me to quickly understand the system in place. OpenStreetMap’s finer details in their maps allowed for an ease of use, especially with layers you are capable of changing. The simple but effective user interface allows for anyone to use OpenStreetMap effectively. I went to Midleton on the map and noticed a lot of details were missing or outdated. There were missing restaurants, statues, shops and more. I had added the points in myself and left them up for reviews for other users.
